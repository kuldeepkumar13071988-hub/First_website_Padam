Chapter 3: Tissues in Action

Understanding the Natural Biological Processes

Introduction

Life begins when a single cell divides itself several times to give rise to a large number of cells. These cells gradually form the skin (protection), muscles (movement), bones (support), nerves (control and coordination), and all other organs. This process is so intricate that it is considered one of nature’s greatest engineering marvels.

A tissue is a group of cells (similar in structure) that work together to perform a specific function. The formation of different types of tissues leads to the division of labour, which increases the efficiency of the body and enables it to carry out complex life processes.

For example, in animals, muscle tissue enables movement and nervous tissue carries messages to different parts of the body. In plants, conducting tissues, such as xylem transports water and minerals, while phloem transports food.

3.1 Why are Plant and Animal Tissues Different?

Most plants are fixed in one place and do not move from place to place like animals. They need support to stay firm and upright. Plant cells have a cell wall that provides rigidity and strength.

In general, animals can move (although some, such as sponges, are immobile). Without a rigid cell wall, animal cells can change shape easily. This cellular flexibility eventually helps make their bodies suitable for locomotion.

Another major difference between plants and animals is their mode of nutrition. Animals have tissues that help them digest food obtained from different food sources, while plants have tissues that help them utilise solar energy for synthesising the food components through photosynthesis. The growth patterns in plants and animals also vary because the tissues responsible for growth differ in structure and function.

3.2 Tissues for Growth in Plants

Plants grow in different ways:

  • Increase in length (height of stem and depth of roots)
  • Increase in girth (thickness of stem)
  • Regrowth after cutting the branches or grazing by animals.

This growth requires actively dividing cells that together form a tissue called a meristematic tissue.

3.2.1 Apical meristem — How do plants grow in length?

Plants have growth zones at the tips of their roots and shoots, called the apical meristems, which help the plants grow in length. Roots grow only from their tips which consist of continuously dividing cells.

3.2.2 Lateral meristem — How do plants grow in girth?

The increase in girth occurs due to the presence of actively dividing cells arranged in a ring in the stem. These cells divide and produce new cells inside and outside in a concentric manner, leading to an increase in diameter of stem. This meristematic tissue is called the lateral meristem.

3.2.3 Intercalary meristem — How do plants grow after being cut?

The intercalary meristem is located at the base of internode or just above the node. It helps certain plants, such as grasses, regenerate after cutting or grazing.

3.2.4 Permanent tissues

The cells that lose the ability to divide undergo changes in structure and function, and become permanent tissues. This process of specialization is called differentiation.

(i) Protective tissue — Epidermis: Forms the outermost layer of the plant body, protecting it against mechanical injury, water loss, and harmful microorganisms. Covered with a waxy layer called cuticle.

(ii) Supporting tissue — Simple permanent tissues:

  • Parenchyma: Living cells with thin walls, loosely packed. Stores food and performs photosynthesis (in green parts).
  • Collenchyma: Living cells with unevenly thickened corners (due to pectin). Provides support and flexibility.
  • Sclerenchyma: Dead cells with thick walls (due to lignin). Makes structures hard and strong (e.g., coconut husk).

(iii) Conducting tissues — Complex permanent tissues:

  • Xylem: Transports water and minerals from roots to other parts. Contains tracheids, vessels, xylem parenchyma, and xylem fibres.
  • Phloem: Transports food from leaves to other parts. Consists of sieve tubes, companion cells, phloem parenchyma, and phloem fibres.

3.3 Animal Tissues

Like plants, animal cells also group together and specialize in performing different functions. Each tissue performs a specific function.

3.3.1 Epithelial tissues

Forms the outer covering of the body (skin) and lines internal organs. Composed of closely packed cells, preventing entry of germs and reducing water loss.

Function Structure Location
Exchange Single layer of thin, flat cells Blood vessels, lungs
Protection Many layers of cells Skin, mouth, oesophagus
Secretion Cuboidal or columnar cells Salivary glands, sweat glands
Absorption Single layer of tall, pillar-like cells Lining of small intestine

3.3.2 Connective tissue

A tissue that connects and supports other tissues. Blood is fluid, while bone is hard due to matrix differences.

  • Blood: Connects parts by transporting nutrients, gases, etc. Consists of Plasma, RBCs, WBCs, and Platelets.
  • Bone: Hard and rigid matrix. Gives strength, support, and protection.
  • Cartilage: Soft, jelly-like matrix. Provides flexibility and cushions ends of bones.
  • Tendon: Connects muscle to bone.
  • Ligament: Connects bone to bone and provides stability.

3.3.3 Muscular tissue

Carries out movements in our body.

  • Skeletal Muscles: Voluntary control, striated, attached to skeleton.
  • Smooth Muscles: Involuntary control, unstriated, found in stomach and intestines.
  • Cardiac Muscles: Involuntary control, faint striations, found only in the heart.

3.3.4 Nervous tissue

Forms the body's control and coordination network. Cells are called neurons, consisting of a cell body, dendrites, and an axon.

3.4 & 3.5 Musculoskeletal System & Joints

The musculoskeletal system is made up of bones, muscles, joints, cartilage, tendons, and ligaments. It functions under the control of the nervous system to produce movement.

Types of Joints

  • Ball and socket joint: Allows free movement in many directions (e.g., shoulder joint).
  • Hinge joint: Bends and straightens in one direction only (e.g., elbow, knee).
  • Pivot joint: Allows side to side movement (e.g., skull connected to backbone).
  • Fixed joints: Bones cannot move (e.g., bones of the skull).

3.6 Skeletal System

Consists of a framework of bones that provides strength and protects delicate internal organs. Includes the skull, vertebral column (spine), and rib cage.

The rib cage protects vital organs like the heart and lungs, and is joined by flexible cartilage allowing it to expand and contract during breathing.

At a Glance

  • Tissues are groups of similar cells that work together to perform specific functions.
  • Plant tissues are broadly classified into meristematic and permanent tissues.
  • Meristematic tissues allow growth, while permanent tissues perform specific functions like protection, support, and conduction.
  • Animal tissues are mainly of four types: epithelial, connective, muscular, and nervous tissues.
  • Epithelial tissue provides protection; connective tissue connects and supports organs; muscular tissue produces movements; nervous tissue regulates body activities.
  • In our body, movement occurs by the coordination of muscles and bones under the nervous system's control.
