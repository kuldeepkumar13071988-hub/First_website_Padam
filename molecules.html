Chapter 7: Atoms and Molecules

Understanding Chemical Combinations and Laws

1. Laws of Chemical Combination

Chemical reactions are governed by two important fundamental laws established by Antoine L. Lavoisier and Joseph L. Proust:

Law of Conservation of Mass

States that mass can neither be created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction. The total mass of reactants must equal the total mass of the products.

Law of Constant Proportions

States that in a chemical substance, the elements are always present in definite proportions by mass. For example, pure water (H₂O) always contains Hydrogen and Oxygen in a mass ratio of 1:8, regardless of where the water came from.

2. What is an Atom?

An atom is the smallest building block of all matter. Atoms are very small; they are smaller than anything we can imagine or compare with.

  • Atomic Radius: Measured in nanometres (1 nm = 10⁻⁹ m).
  • Symbols: IUPAC approves names and symbols. Symbols are usually 1 or 2 letters from the element's English or Latin name (e.g., Fe for Iron from Ferrum).
  • Atomic Mass: One atomic mass unit (u) is a mass unit equal to exactly one-twelfth (1/12th) the mass of one atom of carbon-12.

3. What is a Molecule?

A molecule is in general a group of two or more atoms that are chemically bonded together. It is the smallest particle of an element or a compound that is capable of an independent existence.

  • Atomicity: The number of atoms constituting a molecule. For example, Argon is monoatomic (Ar), Oxygen is diatomic (O₂), and Ozone is triatomic (O₃).
  • Molecules of Compounds: Atoms of different elements join together in definite proportions to form molecules of compounds (like H₂O, CO₂).

4. Writing Chemical Formulae

The chemical formula of a compound is a symbolic representation of its composition. To write it, you must know the valency (combining capacity) of the elements or ions.

The Cross-Over Method: Write the symbols of the elements, write their valencies below them, and then cross-over the valencies of the combining atoms.

Water

Hydrogen (H) valency: 1

Oxygen (O) valency: 2

H₂O₁ → H₂O

Carbon Dioxide

Carbon (C) valency: 4

Oxygen (O) valency: 2

C₂O₄ → CO₂

(Simplify the ratio 2:4 to 1:2)

5. Competency Based Questions

Test your conceptual understanding:

Q1. Applying Conservation of Mass

Scenario: In a closed flask, 10g of calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) is heated. It completely decomposes to form 5.6g of calcium oxide (CaO) and a gas (CO₂). How much carbon dioxide gas was produced in this reaction? Explain which law you used to find the answer.

Q2. Interpreting Formulae

Scenario: A student writes the formula for Aluminium Chloride as AlCl instead of AlCl₃.
(a) Identify the error regarding valency.
(b) How does this error misrepresent the fundamental Law of Constant Proportions?
