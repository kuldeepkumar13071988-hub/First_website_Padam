Chapter 1: Cell

The Fundamental Unit of Life

Introduction

While examining a thin slice of cork in 1665, Robert Hooke saw that the cork resembled the structure of a honeycomb consisting of many little compartments. He called these boxes cells. Cell is a Latin word for 'a little room'.

The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of life. All living organisms are made up of cells.

The Cell Theory

The cell theory, formulated by Schleiden (1838) and Schwann (1839), states that:

  • All plants and animals are composed of cells.
  • The cell is the basic unit of life.

The theory was further expanded by Virchow (1855) by suggesting that all cells arise from pre-existing cells (Omnis cellula-e-cellula).

Types of Cells

Based on the complexity of their organization, cells are broadly classified into two types:

  • Prokaryotic Cells: Cells that lack a well-defined nuclear region and membrane-bound organelles (e.g., Bacteria). The undefined nuclear region is called a nucleoid.
  • Eukaryotic Cells: Cells having a well-defined nucleus and membrane-bound organelles (e.g., Plant and Animal cells).

Structural Organization of a Cell

Every cell has three key features:

  • Plasma Membrane (Cell Membrane): The outermost covering of the cell that separates the contents of the cell from its external environment. It is selectively permeable.
  • Nucleus: The "brain" of the cell. It has a double-layered covering called the nuclear membrane. It contains chromosomes, which are visible as rod-shaped structures and contain information for inheritance of features from parents to next generation in the form of DNA.
  • Cytoplasm: The fluid content inside the plasma membrane. It contains many specialized cell organelles.

Cell Organelles

Important cell organelles include:

  • Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER): A large network of membrane-bound tubes and sheets. Rough ER has ribosomes (protein synthesis), while Smooth ER helps in manufacture of fat molecules.
  • Golgi Apparatus: Consists of a system of membrane-bound vesicles arranged parallel to each other in stacks called cisterns. Involved in storage, modification, and packaging of products in vesicles.
  • Lysosomes: The "suicide bags" of the cell. They contain powerful digestive enzymes capable of breaking down all organic material.
  • Mitochondria: The "powerhouses" of the cell. The energy required for various chemical activities is released by mitochondria in the form of ATP.
  • Plastids: Present only in plant cells. Chloroplasts contain the pigment chlorophyll and are important for photosynthesis.
  • Vacuoles: Storage sacs for solid or liquid contents. They are small sized in animal cells while plant cells have very large vacuoles.
