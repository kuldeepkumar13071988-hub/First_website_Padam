Chapter 6: Structure of an Atom

Inside the Building Blocks of Matter

1. Subatomic Particles

For a long time, atoms were thought to be indivisible. However, experiments in the late 19th and early 20th centuries proved that atoms are made of smaller, subatomic particles:

  • Electron (e⁻): Discovered by J.J. Thomson. Negatively charged particle with negligible mass.
  • Proton (p⁺): Discovered by E. Goldstein. Positively charged particle located in the nucleus. Mass is approximately 2000 times that of an electron.
  • Neutron (n): Discovered by J. Chadwick. Neutral particle (no charge) with a mass nearly equal to that of a proton. Also located in the nucleus.

2. Models of an Atom

Thomson's Model of an Atom

Proposed that an atom is a positively charged sphere and electrons are embedded in it like seeds in a watermelon or dry fruits in a pudding.

Rutherford's Model of an Atom

Based on his famous Gold Foil Experiment, where alpha particles were directed at a thin gold foil. He concluded:

  • Most of the space inside the atom is empty.
  • The positive charge and most of the mass are concentrated in a very small volume at the center, called the nucleus.
  • Electrons revolve around the nucleus in circular paths.

Bohr's Model of an Atom

To overcome the drawbacks of Rutherford's model (stability of revolving electrons), Neils Bohr proposed:

  • Electrons revolve in specific, permitted orbits known as discrete orbits or shells (K, L, M, N...).
  • While revolving in these discrete orbits, electrons do not radiate energy.

3. Detailed Example: The Carbon Atom

Let's break down the Carbon (C) atom:

  • Atomic Number (Z): 6 (Meaning it has 6 Protons and 6 Electrons)
  • Mass Number (A): 12 (Protons + Neutrons = 6 + 6)
  • Electronic Configuration: 2, 4. The K-shell can hold a maximum of 2 electrons, and the remaining 4 go into the L-shell.
  • Valency: 4. Carbon needs 4 more electrons to complete its octet (L-shell), making it tetravalent.

Because carbon has a valency of 4, it forms the basis of organic chemistry by forming stable bonds with other atoms!

4. Competency Based Questions

Test your deeper understanding based on the new curriculum framework:

Q1. Analyzing Experimental Data

Scenario: You repeat Rutherford's Gold Foil experiment but use a foil of solid Hydrogen instead of Gold. Will you observe the same results? Why or why not?

Hint: Think about the mass of a hydrogen nucleus (a single proton) compared to an alpha particle.

Q2. Application of Atomic Structure

Scenario: Element 'X' has an atomic number of 10 and a mass number of 20. Another Element 'Y' has an atomic number of 10 and a mass number of 22.
(a) Predict how Element X and Y will react chemically.
(b) What term is used to describe the relationship between X and Y?

Hint: Look at the electronic configuration of atomic number 10 (Neon). Think about isotopes.
